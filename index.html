Where can a Connecticut child reach a book?

Not just where books exist, but where a family can actually get to them. We scored every neighborhood in Connecticut by how easily a child can reach the places that lend and give away books, by car and by public transport (bus and rail).

Same state, two maps. Toggle the modes on the map to see how access changes when a family doesn't have a car.

Why

Books only count if you can reach them

Every child deserves books from birth. A library two towns away helps less than one around the corner. We made that difference measurable.

How

One score for every neighborhood

We counted every kind of place a child finds books, weighted each by how directly it puts a book in their hands, and measured how long it takes to get there.

The gap

Where access depends on a car

The car is the best case: anything transit reaches, a car reaches too, usually faster. So this view sorts neighborhoods by how much you can reach without one: from places where access depends entirely on a car, to the few where transit meaningfully helps, to places where books are scarce by any route.

Transit never reaches even a quarter of what a car does anywhere in Connecticut, so "transit helps" means it chips in, not that it matches the car.

Methodology

How the index is built

From counting places to grouping neighborhoods, in plain language. Five steps and a few honest caveats.

1

Count the places

The index maps every kind of place a child can reach books, and weights each by how directly it puts a book in a child's hands.

Schools are weighted by staffing. A school library counts 1 if no librarian is on staff, climbing to 6 as the student-to-librarian ratio improves.

A public library counts ten times a Little Free Library, because it's free, permanent, staffed, and built for reading.

2

Measure how far

For every neighborhood we measure how long it takes to reach each place, by car and by public transport (bus and rail), and a place's value fades the longer it takes to get there.

Technical: an exponential travel-time decay with β ≈ 0.08 per minute. A place about 9 minutes away counts about half as much as one next door.

3

Add it up

A neighborhood's score is the sum, over every reachable place, of its weight times how close it is. More places, and closer or higher-weighted places, both raise the score.

The same neighborhood, scored two ways: strong by car, thin by transit. That gap is what the index is built to measure.

Numbers above are illustrative. Real scoring uses the project's measured travel-time matrices.

4

Sort into easy categories (bins)

Raw scores are hard to read, so we rank every neighborhood into five equal-size groups (Very low, Low, Moderate, High, Very high), and we do it separately for car and for transit, so each map reads clearly on its own.

Transit has an extra "No transit access" group for neighborhoods nothing reaches by bus or rail. Unpopulated areas (no resident children) are set aside entirely.

An honest caveat: because the two modes are binned separately, a transit "High" is high for transit. In absolute terms a car still reaches far more, almost everywhere.

5

Combine the two modes (the gap categories)

To answer "does getting books depend on having a car?" we run every neighborhood through a two-question check. First: is much reachable at all? The car score answers that, because it's the best case. If little is reachable by car, transit (which is always lower) can't change the verdict. If books are reachable, the second question asks how much you can reach without a car, which is transit's share of the car score. That sorts the rest by how much transit chips in.

A suburb with good roads but no bus is access depends on a car. A remote town where neither reaches is low access by any mode. A dense center where transit does more of the work than almost anywhere else is transit helps most.

For district views we average each neighborhood's score by where children live, then route the district through the same decision tree.

What the index can't tell you

  • It doesn't see access just across the state border, even when that's the closest library.
  • It counts schools as reachable for every nearby child, even though a child attends only their own.
  • Transit bins are relative to transit, not absolute. See step 4.
  • It's one lens on book access, not a ranking of communities.

The index is refreshed as data updates.
