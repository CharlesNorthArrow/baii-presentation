Read to Grow · Book Access Index

How easy is it for Connecticut children to reach books?

At Read to Grow, we believe that every child deserves the opportunity to thrive through the power of words and stories, starting at birth. We also know that access to books varies greatly for children depending on where they live. In order to work towards more equity, we've mapped ease of access for every neighborhood in Connecticut by how easily a family can reach the places that lend and give away books.

Why

Books only count if you can reach them

A library two towns away helps less than one around the corner. We made that difference measurable.

How

One score for every neighborhood

We counted every kind of place a child finds books, weighted each by how directly it puts a book in their hands, and measured how long it takes to get there.

The Index

What the index found

This is the index itself, built from the ingredients you just toured. It starts at the finest grain (every half-square-mile in Connecticut), then pulls back to school districts, the unit most decisions get made in. Scroll, or use the controls to explore.

Detail · by car

Every half-square-mile, by car

Each hexagon is one neighborhood, shaded by how easily a child reaches books if the family has a car. Deep blue is well served; deep red is barely reachable. By car, much of the state does reasonably well.

Detail · by transit

Now the same map, by public transport

The same neighborhoods, scored by public transport (bus and rail). Across populated Connecticut, one in five has effectively no transit route to books at all. These bins are relative to transit itself: even "Very high" here sits far below what a car reaches.

Pull back to the 158 school districts, each averaged by where kids actually live. The texture smooths into the unit funders and boards recognize, and the same pattern holds.

The gap

Getting to books without a car

The index values the whole set of book sources a neighborhood can reach, each weighted by type and by how long it takes to get there. This view reads each neighborhood in two steps. First it asks whether the reachable set is meaningful at all, judged by the car score, the best case. Then, where it is, it grades that neighborhood's transit access against transit across Connecticut. Some neighborhoods give car-free families a real option. Others leave them with almost none. Where the set is thin to begin with, the lever is more book sources, not transit.

Even the strongest transit access reaches a narrower set than a car does, so these tiers are relative to transit across Connecticut, which is modest almost everywhere.
