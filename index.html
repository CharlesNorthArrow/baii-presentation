Where can a Connecticut child reach a book?

Not just where books exist, but where a family can actually get to them. We scored every neighborhood in Connecticut by how easily a child can reach the places that lend and give away books.

Why

Books only count if you can reach them

Every child deserves books from birth. A library two towns away helps less than one around the corner. We made that difference measurable.

How

One score for every neighborhood

We counted every kind of place a child finds books, weighted each by how directly it puts a book in their hands, and measured how long it takes to get there.

Inside

A map, the ingredients, the barriers

First the result. Then a tour of the places behind the number. Then the barriers that keep families from using what's nearby.

What goes into our Book Access Index?

Now that the result has a shape, the rest of the storymap opens up the number — every kind of place where a Connecticut child might find a book, and how much each one counts. Scroll to begin the tour.

Out-of-School

Public libraries: the gold standard

Free. Permanent. Purpose-built for reading. The public library is the highest-weighted access point in the index because it does what no other place does at the same time: lends books for free, to anyone, with a person on hand to help.

Out-of-School

Bookstores: books for sale

Bookstores have books on every shelf. They count, but they count less: cost is a real barrier for many families, and bookstores cluster in places that already have a lot of other access points.

Out-of-School

Museums: where stories live

Children's, art, history, and science museums won't lend you a book, but they put kids inside stories. They count as access points because they do something the index otherwise misses: they make reading feel like a place you go.

Chapter Summary

508 out-of-school access points

Connecticut's out-of-school book infrastructure is dense in some places and thin in others. Public libraries do most of the heavy lifting; bookstores and museums add texture. This is the layer most people picture when they think "books in the community."

In-School

700 schools, no library staff

Nearly half of Connecticut's schools have no one staffed to run a library. The children attend every day. The books — if there are any — have no keeper.

In-School

No librarian, poor library-to-student ratio

These schools have some library resources but no certified librarian and a poor library-to-student ratio. Better than nothing; still far from what research says children need.

In-School

Strong library-to-student ratio

These schools have a certified librarian and a strong library-to-student ratio. A child who attends one of these schools has real, staffed access to books every day.

Chapter Summary

1,494 schools: every shade of in-school book access

Squares scaled and colored by school score reveal a state where in-school book access is dramatically uneven. Where a child goes to school changes how often a book reaches their hands.

Nonprofit

A book for every newborn

Read to Grow's Books for Babies program places a book directly in the hands of new parents at the moment a child is born. The window is narrow and the moment is everything. That's why birthing centers carry one of the highest depth scores in the index.

Nonprofit

Book Banks: books in, books out

Book Banks are exactly what they sound like: places that collect and redistribute children's books at scale, for free. The depth is unmatched, but Connecticut has only seven of them.

Nonprofit

Where Read to Grow's books actually arrive

574 active partner organizations across Connecticut request and distribute Read to Grow books to the families they serve. This is the shape of Read to Grow's reach: not a building, but a network.

Nonprofit

A library on every block

Little Free Libraries are tiny, neighbor-stocked, and unpredictable. There are more than a thousand of them in Connecticut. They aren't a substitute for a public library. They are something else: book access at the scale of a sidewalk.

Nonprofit

Books where families already wait

Laundry Cares puts a reading nook inside a laundromat. A small footprint with a clever insight: meet families where they already spend time, not where you wish they did.

Nonprofit

Families receiving books at home

These 2,340 households are enrolled in Read to Grow’s Books for Kids program — requesting and receiving free books directly through a trusted relationship with the organization. They are the most intimate access point in the dataset: behind closed doors, invisible to conventional mapping, but real. Unlike everything else on this map, they represent a relationship, not just a location.

Chapter Summary

4,034 community access points: the network behind the buildings

Stacked together, the nonprofit and community layer is the densest in Connecticut. It is also the most distributed, the most informal, and the most volunteer-dependent, which is the strategic reality the index has to capture.

Childcare

Where the youngest children spend their days

Connecticut’s 1,495 licensed childcare providers (centers, group homes, and exempt programs) serve children full-time at the most formative moment for early literacy. Books are often welcome. A book program is rarely part of the plan.

Childcare

On the map, but not in the index

Family Day Cares are home-based and tiny, typically a handful of children. Connecticut has 1,790 of them. They appear on this map because they belong to the conversation. They carry weight zero today: too small, too varied, too unverified to count toward access. But they are exactly the kind of place where a single shelf could change a year of a child’s life.

Chapter Summary

3,285 childcare locations: books often welcome, not always present

Childcare is the largest family of access points in the dataset and the lowest-weighted on average. That tension is the strategic insight: huge reach, shallow depth, enormous opportunity for any program that can deepen book access in places that already have the children.
